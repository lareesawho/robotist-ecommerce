02 — Problem

Why the Market
Needs This.

The robotics market is not failing because robots are bad. It is failing because the buying, deployment, and support experience is broken for most businesses. Robotist exists to fix that layer.

Access Gap

No trusted local access point

Australian businesses wanting to deploy robotics have no credible, local, English-first buying and advisory layer. Most manufacturers are China-based, most documentation is technical, and most buyers have no idea where to start.

Trust Gap

Hype without deployment reality

The IFR's 2025 humanoid position paper highlights the tension between market hype and practical deployment reality. Buyers are excited but uncertain. The gap between a robot demo and a robot actually working in a facility, patrol route, showroom, or industrial workflow is enormous.

Support Gap

The support experience isn't broken — it's vacant

The experience doesn't fail because it's bad. It fails because it doesn't exist. There is no local integration layer, no repair pathway, no one to call. Businesses are left managing Chinese-language warranty claims and flying in technicians. The vacancy is the opportunity.

03 — Solution

What Robotist
Actually Does.

Robotist is the trust layer between Chinese robotics supply and Australia–New Zealand demand. Not a manufacturer and not a generic reseller, but a curated commerce, deployment, and support platform for buyers who need robots to work in the real world, not just look impressive in a demo.

Year 1 Model

Capital-light commerce plus services

Robotist combines curated hardware sales with secondary development, after-service, rentals, and rollout support. The company stays capital-light by avoiding heavy owned inventory while monetizing the trust, deployment, and customer-success layer around each sale.

Longer-Term Direction

Deployment and monetization platform

As the model proves out, Robotist expands into a deeper platform with local support capacity, training, rentals, secondary market infrastructure, and stronger integration workflows that make robotics easier to deploy, monetize, service, and resell.

Supply

Direct China access

Robert's 21 years in China translate into direct manufacturer access, relationship depth, and faster commercial coordination at source. That gives Robotist a structural advantage in sourcing, pricing, and supplier responsiveness.

Brand

Premium positioning

Robotist is not a grey-market importer. It is a curated, brand-first platform. Lareesa's neuro-branding background means the customer experience is designed to build trust from first contact — not just close a transaction.

Support

Local deployment layer

The plan is to build a Sydney-based support, training, and deployment layer — starting with contractors and technical partners, then maturing into a dedicated team that can handle rollout quality, after-service, and customer success.

the Opportunity.

Ideal Customer 1

Hotels, venues & hospitality — humanoid and quadruped

Hotels, restaurants, event venues, and hospitality operators are the clearest near-term market. A humanoid deployed in a lobby or restaurant creates an immediate wow-factor response — driving guest engagement, differentiation, and earned media. The reality right now: the clients who move first are the ones who understand that commanding the room is a competitive advantage. Deal sizes typically run A$30K–120K per unit.

Ideal Customer 2

Security, facilities & patrol — quadruped-capable

Security company owners and facility managers running perimeter checks, remote monitoring, and patrol automation. Quadrupeds are already commercially proven for this use case, with lower deployment risk, measurable labor cost reduction, and deal sizes in the A$80K–250K range. Clear ROI makes the buying conversation easier.

Ideal Customer 3

Retail, events & showrooms — humanoid brand activation

Retail flagships, brand activation events, and showroom operators who want humanoids for greeting, lead capture, and presence. Humanoids are brand assets here — the goal is to own the room, not run a process. This segment grows with product availability and grows fastest with clients who already understand the value of spectacle.

Australia Takeaway

Consumer robotics drives speed; commercial robotics drives margin

The strategic implication for Australia is clear: consumer robotics can accelerate attention and top-of-funnel demand, while commercial deployments and services create larger ticket sizes, stronger margins, and more defensible recurring value.

Global Humanoid Market

A fast-scaling category with room for multiple winners

The broader humanoid category is expanding rapidly, while Asia Pacific remains one of the fastest-growing regions. That makes local trust, support, and deployment capability increasingly valuable as hardware availability improves.

06 — Business Model

How Robotist
Makes Money.

The model is deliberately capital-light but broader than brokerage alone. Revenue comes from hardware sales, secondary development, after-service, rentals, and secondary-market activity, with services designed to lift blended margin over time.

Revenue Stream 1

Hardware sales

Robotist earns margin on curated hardware sales across consumer and business robotics, acting as the trusted commercial layer between manufacturers and local buyers.

Revenue Stream 2

Secondary development projects

Workflow mapping, SDK/API customization, rollout design, and systems integration create higher-margin project revenue around each robot deployment.

Revenue Stream 3

After-service contracts

Ongoing support, training, warranty coordination, and customer-success services create defensible recurring revenue after the initial transaction.

Revenue Stream 4

Rentals and RaaS-lite

Rental, event, and pilot structures reduce adoption friction and give Robotist a flexible commercial offer for buyers who want proof before full purchase.

Revenue Stream 5

Salvage and secondary market

Refurbishment, buy-back, resale, and secondary market activity extend monetization beyond first sale and support longer-term margin resilience.
